## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Over approximately sixteen months (December 2024 – April 2026), Robert "Grizzly" Hanson — a retired North Texas paramedic, SSDI recipient, traumatic brain injury survivor, and founder of GrizzlyMedicine Independent Research Lab — experienced documented, repeated ADA accommodation failures across three major telecommunications carriers: **T-Mobile**, **Spectrum**, and **AT&T**. Each incident is independently documented with recordings, transcripts, and correspondence preserved in a public GitHub repository.

The pattern is not incidental. Across all three carriers, the same failure mode repeats:

1. A blanket credit-check barrier applied without disability exception — credit that was destroyed by the disability itself (33 months of homelessness during disability benefit determination)
2. ADA disclosures dismissed or ignored at every customer-facing level
3. No accessible accommodation pathway from front-line agents to compliance teams
4. Circular runaround between the carrier and affiliated partners
5. Medical devices — phone, Apple Watch (cardiac/neurological monitoring), tablet — treated as luxury consumer goods rather than assistive technology

This is not a customer service failure. This is a structural, systemic failure to implement ADA Title III at the operational level — replicated identically across T-Mobile, Spectrum, and AT&T.

## INCIDENT 1 — T-MOBILE (Two Separate Failures: December 2023 + Late June/July 2025)

### Failure A — Pre-Service ADA Contact Ignored (December 2023)
Before signing up with T-Mobile, complainant contacted T-Mobile to request service under reasonable accommodation. **T-Mobile never responded.** No interactive process was initiated. No accommodation analysis was performed. Complainant received total silence and was forced to sign up with Spectrum instead.

This is a documented failure under 42 U.S.C. § 12182(b)(2)(A)(ii) — T-Mobile failed to engage the interactive process with a **prospective customer** seeking accommodation. The obligation to provide reasonable modification applies to service access, not just existing customers.

### Failure B — In-Store Mockery and Denial (Late June / Early July 2025)
After months of documented failures with Spectrum (see Incident 2), complainant paid out of pocket to leave Spectrum and join T-Mobile. He visited a T-Mobile retail store to obtain an iPhone, Apple Watch Ultra 2 (medical monitoring), and tablet per promotional terms under which he had recently joined. He had disclosed his disability and the medical necessity of these devices. He was accompanied by his service animal.

**Note on sequence:** Failure A (T-Mobile ghosting) preceded Failure B (Spectrum service period) by approximately 18 months. Complainant only became a T-Mobile customer *after* exhausting Spectrum. The in-store incident occurred at that point, not at initial signup.

### Documented Violations

**A. Public Humiliation and Mockery**
Store employee(s) — and, by complainant's account, multiple staff members — laughed at complainant while he explained his disability status and ADA rights. The laughter was not incidental; it was in direct response to complainant's formal advocacy of his legal rights. This constitutes harassment in a place of public accommodation under ADA Title III.

**B. Denial of Reasonable Accommodation — Medical Devices**
Devices required for medical monitoring (Apple Watch Ultra 2, iPhone) were denied under a credit-check policy applied without any disability exception. Complainant's credit was damaged as a direct and documented result of his disabling injuries and the 33-month SSDI determination process. The credit barrier is a facially neutral policy with demonstrably discriminatory effect on a defined class of disabled individuals.

**C. ADA Disclosures Dismissed**
Complainant made multiple on-record ADA disclosures and specifically invoked Title III reasonable accommodation. The store manager redirected the conversation mid-explanation, saying: *"Sir, sir, sir — I understand you're trying to tell me something, but I want to get us back on task here. Now your deposit is going to be $600. Do you have that or no?"*

**D. Comparison of Medical Device to Consumer Electronics**
Store manager explicitly compared complainant's medically necessary Apple Watch to "buying a new television" — directly rejecting the medical necessity argument and demonstrating a failure to engage with the substance of the ADA request.

**E. Dismissal of Service Animal Context**
Complainant's service animal was present. When complainant became physically ill from the stress of the interaction (consistent with his TBI), he was forced to excuse himself from the store. The environment created by the store's conduct was not compatible with the safety needs of a person with his documented disability profile.

### Partial Resolution
T-Mobile Executive Office (Erika Mares, Sr. Specialist, Team CEO) reached complainant and applied $176 in credits. A subsequent $145 goodwill gesture was offered toward the Apple Watch Ultra 2 but the underlying accommodation failure — the credit barrier applied without disability exception — was not resolved. No formal ADA accommodation process was offered.

## INCIDENT 2 — SPECTRUM MOBILE (Late 2023 – Mid-2025; Full Timeline Below)

### Summary
Complainant transferred to Spectrum Mobile under a promotional offer. Over approximately four months, he experienced repeated service suspensions, denial of device upgrades, refusal of ADA accommodations, and — most critically — a service suspension occurring less than 24 hours after a formal ADA request was submitted, which complainant identified as potential retaliation.

### Documented Violations

**A. Credit Barrier — Same Pattern as T-Mobile**
Identical mechanism: credit-check policy applied uniformly without disability exception, blocking device upgrades and promotional enrollment despite the promotional terms under which complainant joined. Same root cause: credit damaged by disability-related homelessness.

**B. Loss of Medical Monitoring Device — No Accommodation**
Complainant's daughter lost his Apple Watch Ultra 2 during the service transition. His request for a replacement under the "upgrade anytime" promotional terms and/or ADA accommodation was refused. He was without a medical monitoring device for an extended period, creating documented health risk (24/7 cardiac and neurological monitoring gap).

**C. Service Suspension — Potential Retaliation**
Spectrum suspended complainant's mobile service less than 24 hours after a formal ADA accommodation request was submitted. Complainant documented this on the record as potential retaliatory termination of service. A retaliatory service suspension against a disabled individual who has asserted legal rights constitutes a serious additional violation under 42 U.S.C. § 12203 (ADA anti-retaliation provision).

**D. Billing Date Inflexibility — Structural Accessibility Barrier**
Complainant's SSDI payment is distributed between the 27th and 3rd of each month — a payment schedule he cannot control. His auto-pay date was set to conflict with this window, resulting in repeated past-due notices and service suspensions. When he requested his billing date be adjusted to align with his SSDI distribution schedule, this was refused. For a person whose income source and disbursement date are determined by federal law, this represents a failure to provide a reasonable modification to a facially neutral billing policy.

**E. Multiple Requests for Compliance Transfer — Deflected**
Complainant explicitly identified himself as a disabled individual and requested transfer to Spectrum's compliance department. He was transferred to billing specialists and leadership team members, none of whom had authority to address ADA accommodation requests or who engaged with the substance of his claims.

### Partial Resolution
None documented. Complainant ultimately walked away from the final call (Kristen, leadership team) without resolution.

## INCIDENT 3 — AT&T (November 2025 – Present; Incident: April 6, 2026)

### Summary
Complainant contacted AT&T Business Wireless chat support at 8:50 AM to obtain a return shipping kit for an Asurion insurance claim device. The 50-minute exchange (transcript #7682694372624512910) documents six on-record ADA disclosures, five direct refusals to provide ADA compliance contact information, three refused accommodation requests, and a floor supervisor who directed a TBI patient to a web-based dispute form as the offered "accommodation."

### Documented Violations

**A. Six ADA Disclosures — No Accommodation Response**
Complainant made his disability status and ADA rights explicit at 9:04, 9:09, 9:14, 9:15, 9:18, and 9:19 AM. No accommodation was offered at any point. No escalation to an ADA compliance contact was initiated.

**B. Five Refusals to Provide ADA Compliance Contact Information**
At 9:17, 9:27, 9:27, 9:33, and 9:38 AM, complainant made direct, unambiguous requests for AT&T's ADA compliance team contact information. Every request was refused, deflected, or redirected. 28 C.F.R. § 36.104 requires ADA coordinators to be designated and accessible. AT&T's customer-facing agents having no pathway to provide this information is a systemic failure of the regulation.

**C. Three Accommodation Requests — All Refused**
1. Mailed return shipping kit — refused
2. Waiver of $270 non-return charge — refused
3. Drop-off authorization at local AT&T retail location — refused

**D. Web Form Directed to TBI Patient as "Accommodation"**
Floor supervisor "Blaine" directed a patient with documented traumatic brain injury and fine motor nerve damage to a written, navigation-heavy web form to dispute the charge. This is not a reasonable accommodation. Under 28 C.F.R. § 36.303, effective communication with people with disabilities requires providing appropriate auxiliary aids and services. A web form is per se inaccessible to someone with the complainant's documented cognitive and motor profile.

**E. Dismissal of Legal Documentation Notice**
When complainant stated he was logging the exchange for legal purposes, floor supervisor Blaine's complete response was: *"Sure, Robert."*

**F. AT&T/Asurion Circular Runaround**
Each organization directed complainant to the other. Blaine ultimately confirmed (9:35 AM) that Asurion can provide a free RMA box — information that required 45 minutes of advocacy to extract and was never proactively offered.

**G. FirstNet and Federal Funding Obligations**
AT&T holds the FirstNet contract (national broadband for first responders) and participates in FCC E-Rate. Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act attaches. Complainant is a former first responder. AT&T benefits directly from federal programs that serve the professional successors of people like him.

## THE COMMON MECHANISM — CREDIT AS A DISABILITY TRAP

The most legally significant pattern across all three incidents is the use of the credit check as a blanket barrier without disability exception.

Robert Hanson's credit was damaged by a specific, documented causal chain:
1. He was injured in the line of duty serving the citizens of North Texas
2. The SSDI determination process took 33 months
3. During those 33 months, he lost his home, his vehicle, and all possessions
4. His credit was destroyed by the financial impact of the 33-month wait
5. The credit score is now used by T-Mobile, Spectrum, and AT&T as justification for denying him the very assistive technology he needs to manage the disability that destroyed the credit

This is a closed loop of systemic discrimination. The disability causes the credit damage. The credit damage causes the technology denial. The technology denial worsens the disability management. It is not an accident that this pattern repeats across three carriers — it is the predictable result of an industry-wide credit-check policy that has no ADA exception built in.

Under ADA Title III, 42 U.S.C. § 12182(b)(2)(A)(ii), a public accommodation must make "reasonable modifications in policies, practices, or procedures, when such modifications are necessary to afford such goods, services... to individuals with disabilities." A credit policy applied with zero disability exception fails this standard when applied to an individual who can document that the credit damage was caused by the disability itself.

## SECTION IV — CORROBORATING EXTERNAL PATTERN
### Industry-Wide Evidence of Systemic Non-Compliance

The incidents documented above are not outliers. Research into public complaints, court records, EEOC filings, and consumer forums confirms that each carrier involved in this case has an independently documented history of ADA accommodation failures operating through the same mechanism.

---

### AT&T / BellSouth — Active EEOC Litigation (2025)

The EEOC filed suit against BellSouth Telecommunications, LLC (AT&T subsidiary) in January 2025, alleging that a blanket 275-pound weight limit applied to field employees violated the ADA by failing to provide individualized disability assessment or accommodation. *EEOC v. BellSouth Telecommunications, LLC* (U.S. District Court, E.D. Louisiana, 2025).

The mechanism is identical to Complainant's case: a **facially neutral blanket policy applied without an individualized disability exception**. AT&T is currently a defendant in active federal ADA litigation for precisely this structural failure. The company was therefore **on notice** of its systemic ADA compliance gaps at the time of the April 6, 2026 incident.

**Source:** https://www.eeoc.gov/newsroom/eeoc-sues-att-bellsouth-disability-discrimination

---

### Spectrum / Charter Communications — Settled EEOC Case + Ongoing Litigation

**Charter Communications (Spectrum) settled an EEOC disability discrimination lawsuit**, paying $60,000 in damages after it:
- Granted a reasonable schedule accommodation for a disabled employee
- **Revoked it the next month** with zero interactive process
- Told the employee that "assistance with your commute is not required under the ADA"

The Seventh Circuit reversed the initial district court ruling against the employee and found Charter in violation. Charter paid $60,000 and agreed to modify its employment practices.

**Source:** https://www.eeoc.gov/newsroom/charter-communications-pay-60000-eeoc-disability-discrimination-lawsuit

This settlement is directly relevant to Complainant's Spectrum incident: Spectrum has an established pattern of **granting accommodations and then revoking them**. Spectrum's service suspension of Complainant's account less than 24 hours after a formal ADA request is consistent with this documented organizational behavior. The EEOC's own case against Charter established that Spectrum has structural ADA process failures and has previously agreed to reform its practices — a reform it apparently did not implement at the customer-service level.

Additional pending Spectrum ADA cases: *Fulks v. Spectrum/Charter Communications* (UniCourt); *Eyth v. Spectrum Charter Communications* (Casetext). The litigation landscape confirms this is not a resolved compliance issue.

### T-Mobile — Documented Toxic Retail Culture; Forced Digital-Only Policy

T-Mobile's retail stores have been independently documented as operating under extreme sales pressure that overrides accessibility and dignity considerations. Investigative reporting (TMO Report, July 2024) documented that "shady T-Mobile stores" trained employees to delete complaint chat logs, apply high-pressure sales tactics regardless of customer circumstances, and prioritize sales metrics above all else. Employees reported management threats for failing to meet sales targets. https://tmo.report/2024/07/look-at-the-ways-these-shady-t-mobile-stores-were-treating-customers-and-employees/

Additionally, T-Mobile has implemented a policy forcing all in-store activations and upgrades through the **T Life app** — a digital-only, navigation-heavy self-service interface. Customers without smartphones, customers with cognitive disabilities, and customers with fine motor impairments have reported being unable to complete in-store transactions as a result, with employees unable to override the system. https://www.phonearena.com/news/T-Mobile-customers-are-walking-out-because-of-stupid-policy-but-employees-are-getting-castigated_id168117

For a complainant with TBI and fine motor nerve damage, a mandated digital interface is a structural accessibility failure. The T Life app policy, combined with the extreme sales culture documented in the July 2024 investigation, creates an environment in which Complainant's December 2025 experience — dismissal, mockery, refusal of accommodation — is not aberrant. It is the **predicted output of a documented institutional culture**.

### The SSDI Credit Trap — Recognized Legal Vulnerability

The legal and advocacy community has identified the credit-check barrier as a recognized potential ADA Title III violation when applied to SSDI recipients whose credit was damaged by the disability or disability-determination process:

> *"If a telecom policy disproportionately impacts disabled customers and the company cannot justify it as necessary, there may be an ADA violation."*
> — FindLaw, "Disability Access: How to File an ADA Title III Complaint" (2024)

The Supreme Court has established that SSDI status does **not** bar ADA discrimination claims. *Cleveland v. Policy Management Systems Corp., 526 U.S. 795 (1999)*. Carriers cannot argue that a person receiving SSDI has somehow forfeited ADA protection.

Under 42 U.S.C. § 12182(b)(2)(A)(ii), a public accommodation must modify policies "when such modifications are necessary to afford such goods, services... to individuals with disabilities." A credit policy applied with **zero disability exception** fails this standard when:
1. The credit damage is causally linked to the disability (SSDI determination homelessness)
2. The goods requested are assistive technology / medical monitoring devices
3. The carrier cannot demonstrate the blanket policy is necessary for service provision

The SSDI-to-credit-to-device-denial feedback loop Complainant has experienced is a **legally recognized pattern** — acknowledged by the DOJ, disability rights attorneys, and the advocacy community as the correct target for ADA disparate impact claims.

To calibrate the financial stakes: Verizon Communications settled an EEOC ADA disability discrimination case for **$20 million** for failure to accommodate disabled employees. This is the benchmark for ADA enforcement outcomes against major telecommunications carriers.

AT&T, T-Mobile, and Spectrum are all on notice — through their own litigation histories, the Verizon precedent, and public advocacy — that ADA non-compliance at scale carries material legal and financial risk.
